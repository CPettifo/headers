Your Request Headers Tell a Story

A good amount of information is given away in a request header, which includes details like your browser, operating system, language settings, IP address, and other metadata. This information is normally used to optimise functionality and user experience (for example serving region or language-specific content), but it can also reveal personal data about you. Your headers can be used to link your browsing habits to you even if you block trackers, reject cookies, and use a VPN!

Browser Fingerprinting


Your Request Headers Tell a Story

A good amount of information is given away in a request header, including details like your browser, operating system, language settings, IP address, and other metadata. This information is normally used to optimise functionality and user experience (for example serving region or language-specific content), but it can also reveal personal data about you. Your headers can be used to link your browsing habits to you even if you block trackers, reject cookies, and use a VPN!

There are identifying characteristics, i.e. when you visit a site if you are "unique" enough, that site will say, hey someone using Chrome visited my site, they spoke English and were in the Beijing timezone, but their location was set to Dublin , they were on Mac and were using Chrome. This information is shared among some places and a site like say, Youtube, Google, Amazon might link this to your login, which tells them, oh Greg logged in using this browser at this time, and that browser matches one that visited Toys-R-Us yesterday. Then that information can be sold to data brokers, so that an online browsing history is built about you, without your consent!


What Your Header Story is Used For

Your journey across the internet is used primarily to serve you more personalised advertisements. If linked to your personal identity, it enriches the information that data brokers collect on you. I'll use an example of Greg, who normally browses using Chrome, where he stays logged in to his social media accounts. Greg is savvy enough to know that if he is shopping for his partner's surprise present (a bookcase) then he may be served personalise ads that could give away the surprise if his partner looks over his shoulder and sees advertisements for bookcases. So Greg decides to use Firefox, and proceeds to purchase the gift, but once he starts using Chrome again he notices that he's starting to get advertisements for bookcases, how could he have been tracked across different browsers?

It can also be used to track your activity, oh Greg is up early this morning, normally he goes onto Facebook at 0730 and then goes over to Instagram, then starts work at 0913. But today he is looking for fancy clothes, so maybe he has a date planned, let's tell him that there are many local singles in his area! Or, looks like Greg browses using this computer on weekends and in the evenings, so that's likely his home computer, also, he seems to be researching a lot about dance classes, so let's make sure that on his socials he sees more posts about dancing. With the example of dancing is fairly innocent, if instead a user was starting to show more interest in nationalism, and wanted their country to only allow for citizens to live there, and for citizens to only be descended from that country, this user may start to see more posts that would confirm these beliefs, which could eventually lead to a feedback loop of the user consuming content that would gradually bring the user deeper and deeper into radicalisation. Social media platforms survive on engagement, and most engaging are feelings of outrage, unfairness, and fear. Is it possible for Greg to be radicalised by an algorithm solely based on his browsing habits?


How to Reduce Your Fingerprint

Unfortunately dealing with fingerprinting is counter-intuitive, steps that you take to try to maintain your privacy like using a VPN, or a less popular browser like Brave, LibreWolf, or even Firefox, make you stand out against the majority of users using Chrome. Heck, if I browse on Firefox on my Ubuntu computer my fingerprint is unique out of over 300,000 other browsers.

Cover Your Tracks does a much better job of explaining this, and has a free tool for testing how identifiable you are in your current browser.
