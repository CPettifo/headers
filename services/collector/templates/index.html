Your Request Headers Tell a Story

A good amount of information is given away in a request header, which includes details like your browser, operating system, language settings, IP address, and other metadata. This information is normally used to optimise functionality and user experience (for example serving region or language-specific content), but it can also reveal personal data about you. Your headers can be used to link your browsing habits to you even if you block trackers, reject cookies, and use a VPN!

Cover Your Tracks does a much better job of explaining this, and has a free tool for testing how identifiable you are in your current browser.

There are identifying characteristics, i.e. when you visit a site if you are "unique" enough, that site will say, hey someone using Chrome visited my site, they spoke English and were in the Beijing timezone, but their location was set to Dublin , they were on Mac and were using Chrome. This information is shared among some places and a site like say, Youtube, Google, Amazon might link this to your login, which tells them, oh Greg logged in using this browser at this time, and that browser matches one that visited Toys-R-Us yesterday. Then that information can be sold to data brokers, so that an online browsing history is built about you, without your consent!


What Your Header Story is Used For

Your journey across the internet is used primarily to serve you more personalised advertisements. If linked to your personal identity, it enriches the information that data brokers collect on you. I'll use an example of Greg, who normally browses using Chrome, where he stays logged in to his social media accounts. Greg is savvy enough to know that if he is shopping for his partner's surprise present - a stuffed panda - he might start seeing personalised ads for stuffed animals. If his partner glances at his screen, the surprise could be ruined.

To avoid this, Greg decides to use Firefox to purchase the gift. However, when he goes back to Chrome, he finds that he starts to see advertisements for stuffed animals. How could he have been tracked across different browsers?

Account-based tracking

Greg could have signed in to his Amazon account to make the purchase, Amazon would've known that Greg was interested in stuffed pandas, when he returned to Chrome his Amazon suggestions would include similar items. Greg used another Amazon account that he only makes gift purchases on, so this was not the cause

At The End of The Day You're Another Day Older

What started as a small portfolio project led to some surface-level research into the world of browsing data and how this information is harvested to be sold to data brokers, and how much information is out there about all users of the internet.

Unfortunately dealing with fingerprinting is counter-intuitive, steps that you take to try to maintain your privacy like using a VPN, or a less popular browser like Brave, LibreWolf, or even Firefox, make you stand out against the majority of users using Chrome. Heck, if I browse on Firefox on my Ubuntu computer my fingerprint is unique out of over 300,000 other browsers.

Cover Your Tracks does a much better job of explaining this, and has a free tool for testing how identifiable you are in your current browser.
